Earth

August 20, 1977

Launching a few weeks prior to Voyager 1, Voyager 2 is launched NASA from the Kennedy Space Center at Merritt Island, Florida. Although it is launched first, it is planned to take an alternate route and is quickly passed by Voyager 1 as it visits the outer planets in more depth.

Jupiter

July 9, 1979

Voyager 2 takes more time to focus on each of the Galilean moons of Jupiter (Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto). It discovers cracks in the surface of Europa, suggesting at liquid water and a deeper ocean on the moon. It also finds that the volcanos of Io are still erupting since Voyager 1's pass months prior.

Saturn

August 26, 1981

Once again, Voyager 2 spends much of its time observing the Saturn's moons. It finds signs of geyser activity on Enceladus, and makes close encounters with the ice moons Iapetus and Tethys, as well as some of the moons Voyager 1 encountered. It also finds hexagonal weather pattern around Saturn's north pole.

Uranus

January 24, 1986

Voyager 2 becomes the first object to observe Uranus and its moons (plus 11 new ones) up close. Whilst there it discovers the planet's magnetic filed is on its side with the poles near its equator. It also discovers a set of very dark rings around the planet as well as its record-breaking low temperatures.

Neptune

August 25, 1989

For the first time, Neptune is captured up close along with its moons (including 6 new ones). A large storm similar to Jupiter's spot called "The Great Dark Spot" is discovered, as well as some of the highest wind speeds in the Solar System. Triton is also photographed, and geysers are found erupting near the moon's south pole. Beyond Neptune, Voyager 2 begins its journey into the outer edge of the Solar System.

Triton

November 5, 2018

Voyager 2 has crossed into interstellar space, the second man-made object to do so. This means it is no longer under the protection of the heliosphere, the zone under the Sun's magnetic field. The Voyager missions have provided decades worth of knowledge about the outer reaches of our Solar System and were a benchmark for future satellites and voyages taken up by NASA.
